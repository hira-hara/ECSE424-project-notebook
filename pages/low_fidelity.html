Design Decisions

  • Round vs square
    • We decided on the squared screen design because of hardware limitations for now.
  • App vs no app
    • We decided to have a no app approach since having an app to control some of the features would defeat the purpose of our goal. We would rather have our users have minimal context switching.
  • Touch screen vs no touch screen
    • We ultimately decided on no touch screen because our mirror’s original idea is to give the user information. We were torn between adding it for the users’ TODO list but we decided it is better for the user to not waste time on clicking the tasks they completed.
  • Using a remote
    • With our no app approach we thus chose to use a remote to interact with the system. It is easy to set up technically, just needs a cheap sensor, and TV remotes are very accessible and familiar to many.

Prototype 1:

This design of our first prototype contains the following features:

  1. Date and Time displayed in top center, similar to how phones display the time
  1. TODOs for the day are resented to the left of the time in the left corner
  1. Weather information is presented on the top right corner to the right of the time.
  1. Extendable/retractable stand to prop up the mirror
  1. Hinge on the back for optional mirror attachment
  1. USB-C power plug below the hinge to connect it to electricity
  1. Settings option on the screen to be able to access the settings option
  1. Bezels/ a frame for structural reinforcement so that the mirror is more durable

The information was chosen to be placed on the top bar of the mirror with the logic of the information being close to each-other makes it easier to find, and frees up the rest of the mirror for unobstructed mirror use. The placement of the date and time was done in a way aiming to be familiar to most users, as it adheres with most computer and smartphone placements of the date and time.


This prototype’s design is to be always on when plugged in.

Prototype 2:

This design of our second prototype contains the following features:

  1. Date and Time displayed in top left
  1. TODOs for the day are presented on-command
  1. Weather information is presented on the top right corner to the right of the time. Weather information also lists city location.
  1. Portable stand to prop up the mirror
  1. USB-C power plug on the lower back.
  1. No settings option on the screen, long press remote instead.
  1. LED light over bezels.
  1. Power button

Prototype 2 aims to create a calm and minimalist experience by limiting persistent on screen elements and keeping essential information easily accessible. The long press settings access preserves visual simplicity, while the LED light enhances usability during grooming. Its portable design allows flexibility in placement, and the balanced layout supports intuitive morning interactions without overwhelming the user.

Usability Goals and Benchmark Tasks:

Usability Goals

We will determine usability goals to evaluate our calm smart mirror design via systematic testing. These goals will evaluate how effectively the system integrates into users’ daily routines while also adhering to calm technology principles. The goals focus on ensuring the mirror delivers value via ambient awareness, minimal cognitive load, and reliable passive operation. The goals are grounded in Nielsen’s 10 Usability Heuristics.

  1. Aesthetic and Minimalist Design:
    1. The display must contain only essential information- date, time, weather summary (rain chance, high/low temps), and maximum 5 calendar events for the day- with zero unnecessary visual features.
    1. We will conduct preference testing comparing minimalist designs, use user feedback to verify if visual hierarchy guides attention to priority information first, and gather feedback on whether any elements feel unnecessary or distracting during morning routines.
    1. Rationale: This is our highest priority because it defines the entire philosophy of calm technology. A cluttered mirror defeats its purpose, becoming another attention demanding screen instead of a peripheral information source. Every additional element competes for attention. The mirror should enhance the space, not clutter it with unnecessary information. 
  1. Familiarity:
    1. Users should instantly (within 10 seconds) recognize what information is displayed and understand its meaning through familiar conventions- clock formats, weather icons, and task list structures they’ve encountered in existing applications.
    1. All information should be self-explanatory: weather should use universally understood symbols (sun icon, rain drops, temperature degrees), tasks should appear as readable text with clear time indicators. No codes or abbreviations should require memorization.
    1. We will observe first-time users interpreting the display without instruction measuring whether they correctly identify all six information types within 10 seconds, track conduction about any symbols or indicators, and test comprehension across diverse age groups and technical backgrounds to ensure accessibility.
    1. Rationale: Our mirror is used during rushed, half-awake forming routines when cognitive resources are limited. If users must think about what they’re seeing, they’re not glancing, but reading and processing, which breaks the calm technology model. This connects to Nielsen’s usability heuristic of “Recognition Rather Than Recall”.
  1. User Trust
    1. Users must be able to understand whether displayed information is current- specifically when weather data and TODO/calendar items were last updated- within 5 seconds of viewing the display.
    1. The system should provide subtle, non-intrusive indicators showing sync freshness.
    1. We will measure how quickly users can determine if weather and tasks are current, track how often they question data accuracy, and test whether the update indicator is visible yet unobtrusive.
    1. Rationale: If users can’t trust that the information they are seeing is fresh, then they wouldn't trust relying on the device for their morning routines. This connects to Nielsen’s heuristic of “Visibility of System Status”.
  1. Error Prevention:
    1. The system must prevent and gracefully handle common errors without user intervention: network connectivity losses, API failures, sync conflicts, or data corruption should not result in frozen displays, misleading information, or stale data being presented as current.
    1. We will simulate various failure modes (disconnected network during sync, corrupt weather API response, Google Calendar service timeout), measure whether the system recovers automatically without manual intervention, and track whether error indicators are noticeable yet non-alarming. Users should never need to “fix” or restart the mirror.
    1. Rationale: Calm technology should be invisible when working correctly.
  1. Use the Mirror as a Mirror
    1. Users can successfully use the device as a functional mirror while information is displayed (the primary function shouldn't be compromised). Visual information should be understandable without overpowering the mirror's reflection, and users should be able to comfortably use it for grooming, outfit checks, and other typical mirror activities. The mirror should be easily integrated into their lives through an appropriate physical form—having a stand or wall mount option and being adequately sized for typical bathroom/bedroom placement.
    1. We will test this by observing users performing standard mirror tasks (checking appearance, adjusting clothing, simulating grooming activities like checking teeth/hair) while information is actively displayed. We will measure if they naturally use it for both purposes at the same time, or favor one function over the other. We will gather feedback on display brightness and reflection clarity across different lightning conditions (ie: bright morning, dim evening). We will also gather feedback on the size of the mirror, and asses if the user would be willing to integrate the mirror into their furniture.
    1. Rationale: A mirror that can't properly show your reflection becomes a screen with a poor viewing angle. Further, if the physical form factor doesn't fit naturally into existing spaces (i.e- too large for a bathroom counter, too heavy to mount safely, lacking appropriate stands) it becomes an intrusive piece of technology demanding accommodation instead of ambiently enhancing the surroundings. The mirror must earn its place in users' homes by being an excellent mirror first, with information display as a secondary feature. This connects to Nielsen’s heuristics of “Aesthetic and Minimal Design” and “User Control and Freedom”.
  1. Personalization
    1. Users must be able to customize essential display preferences (location, time/date/temperature formats, calendar source, widget arrangement) through a simple remote-controlled interface that requires no external devices after initial setup. The setup process should be completable within 5 minutes for first-time users, with settings changes taking less than 1 minute.
    1. The customization interface should only appear when explicitly invoked (long-press on remote), keeping the primary mirror display clean and uncluttered during normal use. All preferences must persist across power cycles without requiring reconfiguration.
    1. We will measure time-to-completion for initial setup and subsequent preference changes, track error rates during navigation, observe whether users can locate and modify settings without instruction, and gather feedback on whether the customization options meet their needs without overwhelming them with unnecessary choices.
    1. Rationale: Calm technology must adapt to individual contexts—a mirror showing Fahrenheit to a Celsius user or wrong timezone information demands attention rather than providing it peripherally. However, excessive customization options contradict minimalism. We balance these by offering only essential personalization that directly impacts information utility, accessed through a hidden-until-needed interface. This connects to Nielsen's heuristics of "User Control and Freedom" and "Flexibility and Efficiency of Use”

Benchmark Tasks

Now that we’ve defined our primary usability goals, we will develop benchmark tasks that test whether the mirror meets these objectives. Users will receive task descriptions that outline what to achieve while not giving them instructions of how to accomplish the tasks. This will let us assess whether our design is intuitive and naturally learnable.

Task 1: View Today’s Priorities

The user must identify their critical tasks for the day while performing their normal mirror routine. 

Subtasks:

  • START
  • Approach the mirror during simulated morning routine (checking appearance, adjusting hair, etc)
  • Glance at display to identify top priority tasks/events
  • Continue their routine without stopping
  • After 20 seconds, recall the identified priorities when asked.
  • STOP

Testing Metrics

  1. Could the user identify where the priorities are located within 5 seconds of viewing the mirror?
  1. Did the user need to stop their routine to read the display or could they absorb information while multitasking?
  1. How accurate was their recall of task/event details (descriptions and times)?
  1. Which information did they notice first? (testing visual hierarchy)
  1. Did the user describe their experience as “glancing” or “reading carefully”?

Usability Goals Tested: 1, 2, 3

This task tests aesthetic and minimalist design (ensuring information is scannable), familiarity (recognizing tasks as being tasks instantly), and user trust (confidence in the information being current enough to act on).

Task 2: Check the Current Time

The user must determine the current time to assess if they are running late for their next commitment.

Subtasks:

  • START
  • Glance at mirror during morning routine
  • Identify the current time
  • Locate their next calendar event and its scheduled time
  • Determine if they need to hurry up or have adequate time.
  • STOP

Testing Metrics

  1. Could the user locate the time display within 2 seconds?
  1. Was the time format (12/24-hour) immediately understandable?
  1. Could the user identify the next time specific event without scanning through all of the scheduled tasks?
  1. How long did the entire decision-making process take (target: under 5 seconds)?
  1. Would the user trust this information enough to act on it without checking their phone?

Usability Goals Tested: 1, 2, 3

This task tests aesthetic and minimalist design, familiarity, and user trust.

Task 3: View the Weather

The user must determine today's weather conditions including temperature range and likelihood of rain to make appropriate clothing and preparation decisions.

Subtasks:

  • START
  • Glance at the mirror when preparing for the day
  • Identify the current weather
  • Identify the high and low temperatures
  • Identify the change of rain/ snow percentage
  • Make a decision about bringing an umbrella
  • Make a decision about appropriate clothing
  • STOP

Testing Metrics

  1. Could the user locate all three weather components within 5 seconds?
  1. Were the weather icons and symbols immediately understandable without explanation?
  1. Could the user translate rain/snow percentage into an actionable decision (bringing umbrella or not)?
  1. Did the weather presentation match their expectations from other apps/formats?
  1. Would they trust this information without verifying on their phone?

Usability Goals Tested: 1,2,3

This task tests aesthetic and minimalist design (weather information clarity), familiarity (recognizable weather symbols and units), and user trust (confidence in forecast accuracy and freshness).

Task 4: Adjust to Different Lightning Conditions

The user must successfully adjust the mirror's display brightness.

Subtasks:

  • START
  • User is prompted to adjust the display brightness
  • User can locate the brightness adjustment button within 10 seconds
  • User adjusts the brightness
  • STOP

Testing Metrics:

  1. Could the user locate the brightness button without instruction?
  1. How long did it take to find and successfully adjust brightness the first time?
  1. Was the button placement intuitive and accessible during normal mirror use?
  1. Did they find the adjustment process disruptive or seamless?
  1. Would they remember how to adjust brightness after the initial discovery?

Usability Goals Tested: 2,4,5

This task tests familiarity (intuitive button location and function), error prevention (easy adjustment prevents eye strain issues), and using the mirror as a mirror (brightness control doesn't interfere with primary mirror function, and gives the user freedom to adjust the brightness so that it can integrate with their environment).

Task 5: Use the Mirror as a Mirror

The user must successfully use the device for typical mirror functions while information is actively displayed, without the information disrupting the user’s mirror routine.

Subtasks:

  • START
  • Approach mirror with information displayed
  • Check appearance and adjust clothing/hair
  • Simulate grooming tasks (checking teeth, fixing hair)
  • Attempt to read displayed information while simultaneously checking reflection
  • STOP

Testing Metrics:

  1. Could the user see their reflection clearly with the display active?
  1. Did they rate the visual balance between information and reflection as satisfactory?
  1. Could they comfortably perform typical mirror activities without turning off the display?
  1. Did they naturally use it for both purposes simultaneously, or favor one function?

Usability Goal Tested: 5

This task directly tests the “use the mirror as a mirror” goal to determine that the smart features don’t compromise the device’s primary feature as being a mirror.

Task 6: Integrate the Mirror into Daily Routine.

The user must position and mount the mirror in their actual living space using available options (hinge, stand, or frame integration) to assess its physical integration into their environment and routine.

Subtasks

  • START
  • Receive mirror
  • Choose preferred mounting method based on their space
  • Position mirror in their bathroom or bedroom
  • Assess whether size, placement, and mounting work with their space
  • Report on aesthetic fit and functional integration
  • STOP

Testing Metrics:

  1. Could the user successfully position the mirror?
  1. Did the mirror size fit appropriately in their chosen space?
  1. Did the mirror's aesthetic design complement or clash with their existing décor?
  1. Did the mirror feel intrusive?

Usability Goals Tested: 1,5

This task tests if the mirror's aesthetic can integrate into their home. It also tests if the mirror can still be used as a mirror, giving its sizing and the information being displayed. Further, it assesses if the mirror’s adaptability features (like stand or hinge) are appropriate.

Task 7: Customize Mirror Settings

The user must personalize the mirror to match their preferences and location without external instruction, using only the provided remote control.

Subtasks

  • START
  • User is told their mirror is displaying weather for the wrong city and in the wrong temperature unit
  • User must access the settings menu using the remote
  • User changes their location to their actual city
  • User switches temperature unit from Celsius to Fahrenheit (or vice versa)
  • User changes time format from 24-hour to 12-hour (or vice versa)
  • User exits settings and verifies changes are reflected on main display
  • STOP

Testing Metrics:

  1. Could the user locate and access the settings menu without instruction within 30 seconds?
  • How long did the complete customization process take (target: under 2 minutes)?
  • Could the user navigate the settings menu using only remote directional controls?
  • Were setting categories clearly labeled and logically organized?
  • Could the user successfully return to the main display without confusion?
  • Did the user express frustration during the process or find it straightforward?

Usability Goals Tested: 2,6

This task directly tests personalization (user control over essential preferences) and familiarity (intuitive navigation and settings structure).

Description of Test Tasks

Task 1: Complete Initial Setup Process

Objective: Test if first-time users can complete the entire setup process (remote pairing, WiFi connection, calendar sync, location selection, date/temperature format preferences) within 5 minutes without external assistance, and assess if each step is self-explanatory and provides adequate feedback.

Instructions: Set up the mirror.

Observers will time the process and note any confusion, errors, or requests for clarification.

Task 2: View Today’s priorities

Objective: Assess if users can quickly locate the to-do list, absorb the information while multitasking, and recall priorities after a brief viewing period.

Instructions: Approach the mirror as if getting ready in the morning. Point to where today's priorities are displayed while checking your appearance. After 20 seconds of occasional glancing, recall the tasks or events you saw without looking at the mirror.

Task 3: Check the current time

Objective: Evaluate whether users can quickly locate the time and date display and understand the format immediately.

Instructions: Point to where the date and time are displayed.

Task 4: View the weather

Objective: Test whether weather information (high/low temperatures and precipitation chance) is clearly displayed using recognizable symbols. This test also determines if the whether displayed can be quickly translated into actionable decisions about clothing.

Instructions: Find and identify the high temperature, low temperature, and chance of rain or snow. Based on this information, decide whether to bring an umbrella and what clothing to wear.

Task 5: Adjust to Different Lighting Conditions

Objective: Determine if the users can locate and use the brightness adjustment control without instruction or assistance, and assess whether this control is intuitive and non-disruptive to normal mirror use.

Instructions: Without guidance, locate the brightness control and adjust the display brighter, then dimmer. Ask the user to explain how they feel about the button placements.

Task 6: Use the Mirror as a Mirror

Objective: Tests if the digital display compromises the mirror's primary reflection function.

Instructions: Use the mirror for normal morning activities like checking your appearance and grooming while the display is active. Attempt to read displayed information in the same time.

Task 7: Integrate the Mirror into Daily Routine

Objective: Test whether users understand the physical mounting options (hinge, stand, wall-mount) and find out if the mirror's size, aesthetics, and setup flexibility would integrate naturally into their bathroom or bedroom environment.

Instructions:

  • Prototype 1, Identify the part of the mirror where you can mount it onto a wall. Also demonstrate how the hinge can convert to a stand so that the mirror can be placed on a surface.
  • Prototype 2, Identify the part of the mirror that uses the stand and recognize that it is removable

Evaluate if the mounting options are intuitive, the size is appropriate for typical spaces, and whether the user would be willing to integrate the mirror into their home.

Task 8: Access and Navigate Settings Menu

Objective: Evaluate whether users can discover and access the settings menu without instruction, and assess if the entry method (visible gear icon vs. hidden long-press) is intuitive and memorable.

Instructions:

  • Without guidance, access the settings menu using the remote control
  • Navigate to the temperature unit setting and toggle between Celsius and Fahrenheit
  • Exit the settings menu and return to the main display
  • After completing the task, explain how you accessed settings and whether you would remember this method tomorrow

Evaluate if the settings menu is better accessed via a gear icon or a appear-on-command style (by holding the enter button on the TV remote for 5 seconds)

Summary of Test Results

Key Insights

  • Prototype 1 outperformed Prototype 2 in discoverability and ease of use, especially for settings access.
  • The long press interaction for pulling up the settings in Prototype 1 consistently frustrated users.
  • Text size and reflection balance remain top usability concerns.
  • Participants want stability and permanence, wall mounted mirror were preferred.
  • Terminology clarity (”Widgets”) and brightness adjustment flow need redesign.
  • Participants prefer to have the option to move “Widgets” to where they please.
  • Participants prefer to have USBC plug on the bottom or the side rather than back.

Design Recommendations

  • Replace all long press functions with visible icons or quick access buttons.
  • Enlarge text and increase contrast, prioritizing glance-ability.
  • Reorganize UI elements to corners to maximize reflection area.
  • Simplify brightness adjustment with a one step slider or toggle.
  • Rename “Widgets” to clearer terms.
  • Offer two mounting configurations, stable wall mount and optional stand.
  • Placing USBC power plug on bottom or side instead of on back.
  • Allow users to move around “Widgets” based on their preference.

Summary Conclusions

Both participants completed all tasks successfully, with Prototype 1 showing higher usability and lower frustration. Prototype 2 was visually preferred, but functional improvements particularly around text size, control discoverability, and physical design are essential for the next iteration.
