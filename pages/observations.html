Identifying The Problem

For the past decade, phone usage has encouraged people to constantly be connected to information (be it easily accessible or not), entertainment and with other people. It is common to see many people carry their phones to every room they enter. This closeness to our smartphones has been integrated into our sleeping and waking routines. There are many studies and peer-reviewed studies showing the negative effects of being on your phone before you sleep, specifically how exposure to short-wavelength light before sleeping disrupts one‚Äôs nocturnal melatonin secretion thus impacting sleep, 1(H√∂hn, Hahn, Gruber, Pletzer, Cajochen & Hoedlmoser, 2024). Even the mere presence of our phones can reduce our available cognitive capacity, 2(Ward, Duke, Gneezy & Bos, 2017), which can lead our morning routines to be disrupted leading to weaker concentration and awareness throughout the rest of the day.

‚ÄúAs you transition from sleep to wake, your brain transitions through different brainwave states: from delta waves (deep sleep) to theta waves (lighter sleep) and finally to alpha waves, which are associated with quiet wakefulness.
When you first wake, your brain is in a state dominated by theta waves, which are associated with relaxation and daydreaming and gently stimulating creativity. Reaching for your phone right away disrupts this natural progression.
Overloading with stimulating content first thing can thrust your brain into beta waves, the state associated with focused attention. While beta waves are great for tackling tasks later in the day, experiencing them too early can leave you feeling scattered and stressed, and prime your brain for distraction throughout the day.‚Äù

From our observations and interviews, we have reached an understanding that everyone is inclined to check their phone when they need to access weather, time and daily schedule information but at the same time they find themselves latched onto the phone more than they anticipated since our phone‚Äôs are trying to fight for our time.

Our proposed solution, and our aim for this project, is to develop a non-intrusive, and calm technology that aids in a person's morning/night routine. This device will be a mirror with all the information that a person generally needs when they are starting their day. It will help elevate the need to check one‚Äôs phone first thing in the morning and a mirror is integrated into morning routines offering an unobtrusive start of the day.

Step 3 Naming The Groups

  • The Pressed Scheduler

    This group is focused on efficiency and saving time. They have to deal with multiple tasks in the morning and rush through their routines. Their mornings often feel short and they prioritize getting everything done quickly before heading out.

  • The Digital Native

    This group integrates technology into nearly every part of their morning. From checking their phone to using a voice assistant, they heavily rely on digital tools to stay organized and informed. Their routines are built around convenience and connectivity.

  • The Traditional Routinist

    This group sticks to simple habits. Their mornings often revolve on listening or reading traditional sources. They value familiarity, consistency and straightforward routines without relying on technology.

  • The Active Achiever

    This group starts the day with energy and movement. Whether exercising, stretching or preparing for a productive day, they prioritize physical activity and goal setting. They see the morning as an opportunity to boost motivation and stay disciplined.

Designing at a High-Level

The design architecture components:

Hardware Assembly: We will source and assemble the physical components of the mirror. This includes mounting the HDMI monitor behind the one-way mirror sheet within a wooden frame. The RaspberryPi will be embedded into the frame, and connected to the monitor via the HDMI cable. All components will be wired to the power supply.

User Interface: We will use Figma to design the UI that appears on the mirror. This means creating a minimal, calm layout showing time, weather, and reminders with appropriate typography, spacing, and positioning. The design will prioritize readability from several feet away and avoid distracting elements like bright colors. We will gather feedback from potential users and iterate on the design to ensure that it meets their needs for a distraction-free morning routine.

Display Software: We will develop the core display via using Python on the RaspberryPi.

API Integration: Using Python, we will integrate external APIs to fetch real-time data. The Weather API (OpenWeatherMap) will provide current weather conditions and forecasts. The calendar API (Google Calendar) will retrieve the user‚Äôs daily schedule and reminders. These integrations will run on the RaspberryPi and update the display at regular intervals.

Brightness Control Module: We will implement a Python script to let the user adjust the display brightness. This is so that users can adjust it to their needs, as some users may prefer brighter screens if they are placing the mirror in a bright area, while others may prefer dim screens.

Display Timer Script: We will create a scheduling system that allows the user to control when the display turns on and off. This is to avoid unnecessary light during sleeping hours.

Setup and Configuration: We will develop a simple configuration interface that is easily understandable to allow users to initially sync their calendar, set their preferred weather location, and adjust timing preferences.
