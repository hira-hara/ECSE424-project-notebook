1.4 Observations Based on Usability Goals

[Insert reflections on how the test results map back to your original usability goals from the test plan.]

Goal 1 (Minimalist Design): Achieved, but at the cost of Goal 2.

Goal 2 (Familiarity/Recognition): Partially failed. The hidden "long press" for settings violated this goal. The gear icon in Prototype 1, however, was 100% successful...

1.5 Failure Cases

[Insert a description of the most critical or common failure cases observed. What tasks did users fail to complete, and why?]

Critical Failure: Accessing Settings (Prototype 2). Users looked for a visual icon and, finding none, assumed settings could not be changed. This led to task abandonment for 2 out of 3 participants...

1.6 Summary of Usability Issues

[Insert a high-level summary of the key usability problems identified, perhaps prioritized by severity (e.g., critical, major, minor).]

1. (Critical) Settings are undiscoverable (Prototype 2).

2. (Major) Text size is not legible from a standard distance.

3. (Minor) Terminology for "Widgets" is confusing...

Test Plan Critique

[Insert your critique of Group A's test plan here. What was good about it? What was weak? Was it easy to follow? Did it test the right things?]

For example: The test plan from Group A was comprehensive and clearly outlined its usability goals. The benchmark tasks were well-aligned with these goals, particularly Task 3 (Checking Weather) and Task 6 (Using as a Mirror). The scripts were neutral and avoided leading questions.

However, the plan's time estimates were overly optimistic, leading to our sessions running longer than anticipated. Furthermore, the plan did not include a specific task for error recovery (e.g., "What happens if Wi-Fi disconnects?"), which would have been valuable to observe...

Design Critique

[Insert your critique of Group A's *design* based on your test findings. What design decisions were strong? Which ones were weak and led to the usability issues you found?]

For example: The design's strongest feature is its "calm" aesthetic, which all participants appreciated. The decision to place information in the corners (Modification #3) was validated during testing, as users felt it maximized reflection space.

The design's primary weakness was its over-reliance on "invisible" interactions, such as the long-press for settings. This design choice, while intended to be minimal, directly caused our most critical failure case and demonstrates a conflict between the designers' aesthetic goals and core usability principles like "Recognition Rather Than Recall"...
