Evaluations and Notes:

1. Results and Analysis

1.1 Summary of Quantitative Results

In general, the duration of each task took longer than the expected task. Although for tasks 6 and 7, we got the expected time duration. The first two tasks took the longest and they needed the most assistance from the investigators to complete the task. Every task was completed and each subtask was completed. Core functionality tasks took the expected amount of time and showed that the ease of use for the software interface was easier than the initial setup. The initial setup needing assistance showed that there is an ease-of-use problem for non-technical people who aren’t familiar with GitHub.

1.2 Summary of Qualitative Results

Observer 1: Our first participant is a university student who did have some technical knowledge. They followed the user manual but were stuck on the first task because they were not sure how to clone or download repositories from GitHub. This made it difficult for them to start a flow and could have diminished their wanting to continue the project. With our assistance they continued. They had difficulty choosing a serial port as well since they were not sure which one was the correct one. We assisted them for this step as well. The rest of the steps were easily followed. They understood the concept of the light changing and warnings when they switched tabs. Although they commented multiple times about how bright the light is. The user did not have to refer to the manual for the core functionality tasks such as adding a site and removing it. Although they did not see a coffee mug symbol at first which led them to check the manual and disrupting their flow. This is likely a software issue thus making it fixed would give the proper usability effect of getting ample feedback.

Observer 2: Our second participant is a volunteer (They volunteer in organisations). They rated themselves as not very tech-knowledgeable. They also had difficulty with Tasks 1 and 2, thus requiring assistance from us in order to complete the task. They mentioned that having computer knowledge would definitely help a lot more. Similarly, they progressed through the next parts easily. They identified the buttons and interface layout easily but were confused when they could not see the coffee mug symbol showing that the expectation of the user was not met but once again we believe this is a software problem that once fixed will solve this issue. They also mentioned how bright the led lights were.

1.3 Similarities and Differences Across Participants

Similarities:

  • They both found the first two tasks difficult and frustrating.
  • They both mentioned that the light was too bright and “in their face”.
  • Participants expressed that the interface was easy to use.

Differences:

  • Possibly a technical issue but the led lights turned on at Task 2 for User 1 and for User 2 it turned on on Task 3 although we saw that they chose the correct serial port and followed the steps similarly

1.4 Observations Based on Usability Goals

Effectiveness of reminders (Learnability / Engagement): All participants noticed the pop-up warning when they were in a non-focus tab as well as the lights changing color to yellow. There was no dimming effect mentioned during the tasks and manual but mentioned in this Usability Goal so we are unsure about that.

Awareness of breaks (Efficiency / Error Recovery): The participants noticed the breaks and knew they could change the break times.

Ease of managing focus sites (Learnability / Efficiency): The participants easily added and removed websites to and from the focus list. They found the interface very easy to understand.

Ease of starting/stopping sessions (Learnability / Efficiency): The users easily stopped, started and paused sessions intuitively.

User satisfaction (Satisfaction / Engagement): The users generally found the extension satisfactory, although they mentioned they would not use it as often. They found the light a bit disturbing because it was very bright. They had no comment about motivation.

1.5 Failure Cases

  • For both participants, a “failure” case would be to identify the coffee cup symbol in the first half of the study session. The coffee mug did not appear at first and thus they told us that they did not see it anywhere. This might show the system is unpredictable for this case.
  • For both participants, mentioned before we believe the first step would have failed unless we helped them or would take a considerable amount of time since they would have to search up the internet and understand how GitHub and repositories work.
  • For both participants, they chose the wrong serial port initially and had to go through trial and error since there was no instructions on the manual which port should be selected. Showing that the manual should be a bit more comprehensive for people with minimal amounts of technical knowledge.

1.6 Summary of Usability Issues

High-Priority Issues:

  1. Poor Installation and Setup Documentation
    • The documentation is not suitable for all users in the system’s user population. We as evaluators noticed that the first few steps seemed a bit more technical and our observations justified our assumptions since the participants did need help for them.
  2. Lack of Feedback and Clear Error Messages
    • The system fails to communicate its status, especially during setup. The participants were unsure whether the correct serial port was selected since the light did not turn on until Step 3.
  3. Core Functionality Bug
    • We noted a significant bug where the "Focus on study sites" warning appears even when no study sites are added. This was confusing by the participants and forced the user into a dead end where they must "either have to add the site... or remove the extension" in order to remove the warning sign.

Medium-Priority Issues:

  1. Inconsistent Status Indicator
    • The “coffee mug” indicator which is meant to show the system status is not reliably visible. The users indicated that they did not see a coffee mug at first. This reduces discoverability and requires users to remember contextual cues for their study period.

Low-Priority Issues:

  1. Lack of Flexibility and Customization
    • During the evaluation, one of the participants tried to click on the pomodoro timer (25min) and tried to change the timer to a different time. But they were not successful since the implementation is meant only for 25 minutes. This could be a lack of flexibility for users but we understand that it might have been a hardware/software limitation.
  2. Distracting Visual Design
    • The participants noted that the light was very bright and distracting. For a productivity tool, this distraction undermines its core purpose. Although since this is a prototype, the light will likely be integrated into the whole design in a way that it is covered and not too bright.

1.7 Comments from Users

Participant 1: They found the interface generally easy to navigate and understand. As they usually do not use productivity tools similar to Pomodoro sessions they indicated they were not as focused during the session and would not use this product to study/work. They told us the light was very bright. They also mentioned that a person using this needs “knowledge in computers for sure”.

Participant 2: They found the interface less easy to use than the first participant but understood what each component did such as the breaks and adding/removing sites. Although they use Pomodoro Study using Youtube Videos, they indicated that they would not use this system in their future study sessions.

2. Test Plan Critique

The FocusBrowse test plan is clear, well organized and effectively connects to the system’s usability goals and calm technology principles. It uses two methods, a usability test with real users and a heuristic evaluation by experts, to assess both the practical user experience and the interface quality.

Each usability goal, like effectiveness of reminders, awareness of breaks, ease of controlling sessions, intuitive session control and overall satisfaction were matched with specific and measurable indicators. For example, user feedback awareness (goal 1) was tested through the warning bar and LED lights (Visibility of System Status), while ease of session control (goal 4) was linked to the heuristic of user control and freedom through timing and error tracking. The minimalist and satisfaction goals were measured through post-test Likert ratings, ensuring that every design goal had a clear evaluation method, fulfilling the requirement for a complete and goal driven evaluation plan.

Some improvements were made to better match users’ real skills and behaviors. The updated plan incorporated clearer pre-test instructions, a step by step user manual and simplified terminology in tasks such as LED setup and repository cloning. These changes helped focus the test on usability instead of technical setup. The team also standardized task scripts to keep evaluations consistent and added quantitative benchmarks to make the performance outcomes directly comparable across participants.

The heuristic evaluation parallel to the usability testing complemented each other very well. Expert reviewers evaluated FocusBrowse against Nielsen’s ten heuristics, identifying strengths in consistency and standards and aesthetic and minimalist design while noting moderate issues in error prevention and help and documentation. This method ensured coverage of both user and design centered perspectives, getting better feedback and objective data.

Overall, the revised plan fits the project’s goals very well. It measures the right things, uses appropriate HCI methods, and ensures that the feedback collected is both practical and useful for improving FocusBrowse’s calm, user centered design. The added quantitative criteria and expert review components ensure that resulting data will be both reliable and meaningful for future iterations.
